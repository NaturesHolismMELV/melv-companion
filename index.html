Origins — from fieldwork to framework

The Modified Energetic Lotka-Volterra (MELV) framework did not begin at a desk. It began in the Namibian bush between 1981 and 1983, where Laurence W. Evans observed a persistent and intriguing mutualism between hornbills and dwarf mongooses. The two species forage together, with hornbills providing aerial warning of predators while mongooses flush ground insects — each gaining a benefit that reduces the net cost of coexisting with the other. What Evans noticed was that this arrangement was stable: it persisted across seasons, across different individual animals, and across varying resource conditions. That stability demanded explanation.

The classical Lotka-Volterra competition model, formulated in the 1920s, described interspecific competition well but treated cooperation as an edge case. It offered no thermodynamic grounding — no reason why cooperation should be a stable attractor rather than a fragile coincidence. The MELV framework was developed over the following four decades to address precisely this gap, culminating in Blueprint for Harmony (Cooperation Press, 2026).

The core insight is deceptively simple: whether two species cooperate or compete is determined by a single dimensionless ratio — the i-factor — which compares the cost of interspecific competition to the cost of intraspecific competition. When that ratio falls below 1.0, cooperation is the thermodynamically favoured state. When it rises above 1.0, competitive exclusion dominates. The transition between these two regimes is not gradual — it is a mathematical bifurcation, as sharp as water freezing at 0°C.

Key insight: The hornbill–mongoose system works because each species competes more intensely with its own kind than with the other. Their niche overlap (i-factor) sits well below 1.0, placing the system deep inside the cooperative attractor. MELV provides the mathematical language to express why this is not merely lucky — it is thermodynamically inevitable given the service network they inhabit.

The MELV equations — mathematical foundation

2.1 The core differential equations

MELV is built on a modified Lotka-Volterra competition model. For two species with populations N₁ and N₂:

dN₁/dt = r₁·N₁·(1 − N₁/K₁ − i₁₂·N₂/K₁) dN₂/dt = r₂·N₂·(1 − N₂/K₂ − i₂₁·N₁/K₂) where: r = intrinsic growth rate (per generation) K = carrying capacity (maximum sustainable population) i₁₂ = effect of species 2 on species 1 (interspecific competition coefficient) i₂₁ = effect of species 1 on species 2

The modification from classical Lotka-Volterra lies in what the i-factor represents. In the classical model, α₁₂ is a fixed empirical parameter with no theoretical grounding for its value. In MELV, i is a dynamic quantity that evolves under selection, driven by the service network coupling between species (the Ω-matrix, described in Section 4). This makes MELV a genuinely predictive theory rather than a curve-fitting framework.

2.2 The symmetric case and the bifurcation prediction

For mathematical tractability, and to identify the fundamental bifurcation structure, MELV begins with symmetric species: r₁ = r₂ = r, K₁ = K₂ = K, i₁₂ = i₂₁ = i. This is not merely a simplification — symmetry is the canonical case that reveals the deepest structure, and perturbation away from symmetry can be handled analytically (the threshold shifts slightly, to √(i₁₂ × i₂₁) ≈ 1.0, but the fundamental result holds).

Under symmetry, the system has three classes of equilibria:

Trivial equilibria (always exist): E₀ = (0, 0) — both species extinct E₁ = (K, 0) — species 1 alone at carrying capacity E₂ = (0, K) — species 2 alone at carrying capacity Coexistence equilibrium (exists only when i < 1): E* = (N*, N*) where N* = K(1−i)/(1+i) Biological requirement: N* > 0 requires (1−i) > 0, i.e. i < 1

This is the first mathematical hint of the threshold: coexistence is only algebraically possible when i < 1. But algebraic existence is not the same as stability. A fixed point can exist and yet be repelling — every trajectory near it moves away. Stability requires the much deeper tool of eigenvalue analysis, which Module 1 demonstrates interactively.

2.3 Why the i-factor is dimensionless and universal

One of MELV's key contributions is recognising that the bifurcation at i = 1.0 is independent of the specific values of r and K. Look at the eigenvalue expressions derived in Appendix E:

λ₁ = r(i−1)/(1+i) λ₂ = r(i−1)(1+2i)/(1+i) The sign of both eigenvalues is determined entirely by sign(i−1). r > 0 always, (1+i) > 0 always, (1+2i) > 0 for i > 0. Therefore: threshold is at i = 1.0 regardless of r, K, β, or ε.

This universality — confirmed computationally across 152 parameter combinations (ANOVA F(11,140) = 1.03, p = 0.422) — is the hallmark of a genuine mathematical structure rather than a parameter-specific result. It is analogous to the universality of the Lotka-Volterra coexistence criterion α₁₂·α₂₁ < 1, but MELV provides the thermodynamic grounding for why i should converge toward this threshold under evolution.

Module 1 — eigenvalue stability (Appendix E)

3.1 What eigenvalues tell us about stability

Eigenvalue analysis is one of the most powerful tools in dynamical systems theory. The idea is to ask: if a system is near an equilibrium and we give it a small push, does it return or diverge? The answer is encoded in the eigenvalues of the Jacobian matrix — a matrix of partial derivatives that captures how each variable affects the rate of change of every other variable, evaluated at the equilibrium point.

Lyapunov stability theorem: An equilibrium E* is locally asymptotically stable if and only if all eigenvalues of the Jacobian J(E*) have strictly negative real parts. If any eigenvalue has a positive real part, the equilibrium is unstable. If any eigenvalue has a zero real part, we are at a bifurcation point — the linear analysis is insufficient and higher-order terms determine behaviour.

3.2 Constructing the Jacobian for MELV

For the symmetric MELV system at the coexistence equilibrium E* = (N*, N*) where N* = K(1−i)/(1+i), the Jacobian takes an elegant symmetric form. The derivation proceeds in three steps:

Step 1: Compute all four partial derivatives ∂(dNₖ/dt)/∂Nⱼ and evaluate at E*.

Step 2: Substitute N* = K(1−i)/(1+i) and apply symmetry (r₁ = r₂ = r, etc.).

Step 3: Simplify. The algebra is non-trivial but the result is compact:

J(E*) = [ r(i−1) −ri(1−i)/(1+i) ] [ −ri(1−i)/(1+i) r(i−1) ] This is a symmetric 2×2 matrix with equal diagonal elements. For such matrices, eigenvalues are simply λ = a ± |b|. a = r(i−1) b = −ri(1−i)/(1+i) λ₁ = r(i−1)/(1+i) λ₂ = r(i−1)(1+2i)/(1+i)

3.3 The zero-crossing proof

The elegance of this result is that both eigenvalues share the factor (i−1). Since r > 0, (1+i) > 0, and (1+2i) > 0 for all physically meaningful i, the sign of both eigenvalues is determined solely by the sign of (i−1).

  • When i < 1: (i−1) < 0 → both λ < 0 → coexistence is stable
  • When i = 1: (i−1) = 0 → both λ = 0 → bifurcation point
  • When i > 1: (i−1) > 0 → both λ > 0 → coexistence is unstable

This is a mathematical proof that the bifurcation occurs at exactly i = 1.0. It does not depend on any parameter values. It follows analytically from the structure of the MELV equations themselves. The interactive Module 1 lets you verify this numerically — notice how eigenvalues always cross zero at i = 1.0 regardless of how you set r and K.

Proof summary (Appendix E): The coexistence equilibrium E* of the symmetric two-species MELV system is locally asymptotically stable if and only if i < 1, and unstable if i > 1. The transition occurs at a bifurcation point i = 1.0 where both eigenvalues simultaneously equal zero. This result is exact, analytical, and parameter-independent. Computational validation across 152 parameter combinations confirms the numerical threshold at i = 1.008 ± 0.028, statistically indistinguishable from the theoretical prediction of 1.000 (p < 10⁻¹⁰). □

3.4 What the phase portrait reveals

The phase portrait tab in Module 1 shows trajectories in (N₁, N₂) space — not as time series, but as paths. Each line is a different starting condition. When i < 1, all paths converge toward the single interior fixed point E* — a stable node or spiral. When i > 1, all interior paths diverge from the (now unstable) E* and converge instead toward one of the boundary equilibria E₁ or E₂ — one species competitively excludes the other, with the winner determined by initial conditions. This is Gause's competitive exclusion principle, expressed as a phase-portrait geometry.

Module 2 — the master equation and service networks

4.1 The question Module 1 cannot answer

Module 1 proves that i < 1 implies stable coexistence. But it treats i as a fixed parameter. In real ecological systems, the interaction coefficient is not fixed — it evolves. Species diverge in resource use (character displacement), develop complementary behaviours (mutualism), and build service relationships (cleaning symbiosis, warning calls, nutrient cycling). The master equation describes how i changes over time under these evolutionary pressures.

4.2 The master equation

i₁₂(t) = i⁰₁₂ × (1 − ε × φ(t) × β(t)) where: i⁰₁₂ = initial interaction coefficient (at time t = 0) ε = evolutionary rate (speed at which i can change per generation) φ(t) = perpetuity (evolutionary optimisation maturity within β-context) β(t) = service network suitability (driven by the Ω-matrix eigenvalue)

The three terms in the product have distinct biological meanings. ε captures how fast evolutionary change can occur — a species with short generation times and high genetic variation has higher ε. φ(t) is perpetuity: the species-centric measure of adaptive maturity achieved through evolutionary optimisation over time. It is a slow state variable that accumulates over generations, tracking how far a species has progressed toward optimal survival and reproduction within its current β-context. Critically, high φ does not by itself predict cooperation — the Argentine ant has φ ≈ 1.0 (superbly optimised) yet drives competitive exclusion because β is low. It is the product φ(t) × β(t) jointly that drives i toward the cooperative basin. β(t) is compatibility: set by the eigenstructure of the service exchange network (Ω-matrix), it determines the asymptotic floor to which i can be pulled and how far the system can penetrate the cooperative attractor.

4.3 The Ω-matrix and service network suitability

The Ω-matrix encodes the directed graph of service exchange between species. Entry Ω_ij represents the fitness benefit that species j provides to species i per unit of activity. The dominant eigenvalue λ_max(Ω) captures the maximum sustainable rate of cooperative service flow through the network.

Appendix D demonstrates that λ_max(Ω) is a strong predictor of cooperation outcomes, with R² = 0.9248 explaining variance in final cooperation level across simulated systems (p < 10⁻⁵⁰). This is not a correlation between two arbitrary variables — it is a mechanistic relationship: a network with high λ_max can sustain more service exchange per unit effort, which translates directly into a higher β_service and faster reduction in i.

Biological interpretation of β_service: In the hornbill–mongoose system, β is high because the services are complementary and non-substitutable — hornbills cannot replace mongooses' ground-flushing, and mongooses cannot replace hornbills' aerial surveillance. When services are substitutable (multiple species provide the same benefit), β is lower because each species contributes less uniquely. High complementarity → high λ_max(Ω) → high β → faster crossing of the bifurcation threshold.

4.4 What the trajectory simulation shows

In Module 2, you can see that the i(t) trajectory has a characteristic shape: it starts flat (φ is small, so the driving force is weak), then curves sharply downward as φ approaches saturation, then flattens again as i approaches its asymptotic floor i⁰ × (1 − β). The floor is determined by β alone — it is the minimum i achievable given the service network. If β is too low, the floor lies above 1.0 and the system never crosses the bifurcation threshold, regardless of ε or starting i. This is why increasing β_service is the dominant lever in the sensitivity analysis.

The crossing time — how many generations until i falls below 1.0 — is the key prediction. Systems that cross quickly have high β and moderate initial i₀. Systems that never cross have insufficient service network coupling. This matches Gause's classic experiments: species that coexist in heterogeneous environments (high spatial β) but exclude each other in homogeneous conditions (low effective β).

Module 3 — agent-based equilibrium landscape (Appendix A)

5.1 From analytical to empirical validation

Module 1 provides an analytical proof of the bifurcation. Modules 2 and 3 provide empirical validation — the kind that convinces ecologists and computational scientists who may be sceptical of any model that is too clean. Agent-based simulation is the gold standard for testing whether analytically derived thresholds survive the addition of stochasticity, evolutionary feedback, and parameter heterogeneity.

Appendix A reports results from 10,000 simulated systems, each with independently drawn parameters. The key result is a bimodal distribution of final i-factor values — systems overwhelmingly end up either well below 1.0 (cooperative attractor) or well above 1.0 (competitive regime), with very few in the intermediate zone. This bimodality is the fingerprint of a true bifurcation: if the threshold were merely a soft transition, we would expect a unimodal distribution centred near i = 1.0.

5.2 The ternary landscape

The definitive 10,000-system analysis reveals a ternary equilibrium structure:

  • 78.0% of systems end in the low-i cooperative attractor (i* ≪ 1.0)
  • 16.2% of systems are in the threshold zone (i near 1.0, β-dependent)
  • 5.8% of systems end in the high-i competitive regime (i* > 1.0)

The 78/16/5.8 split is not arbitrary — it reflects the fact that most randomly parameterised competitive systems have sufficient niche differentiation and service coupling to eventually cross the threshold. The 5.8% that remain competitive are systems where initial i₀ is very high and β_service is very low — the two conditions that together prevent crossing, as the landscape map in Module 3 shows directly.

Key statistic: The bimodality of the final i-factor distribution is statistically significant at p = 0.003 (Hartigan's dip test). The correlation between initial i-factor and final cooperation level is r = −0.944 (p < 10⁻⁶). The refined threshold estimate from 10,000 systems is i* = 0.9995 ± 0.029 (95% CI: [0.9976, 1.0014]) — converging on the analytically predicted i = 1.0 with extraordinary precision.

5.3 Reading the landscape map

The landscape map in Module 3 plots each grid cell in (i₀, β_service) space, coloured by the final regime of systems starting there. The separatrix — the boundary between cooperative and competitive cells — is the most important feature. It is not vertical (i₀ alone does not determine outcome) and not horizontal (β alone does not determine outcome). It is a curve in the joint parameter space, reflecting the master equation: you need either low enough i₀ or high enough β, or some combination of both, to achieve crossing.

This is precisely the kind of result that cannot be derived analytically — it requires simulation. The agreement between the analytical threshold (i = 1.0 from Module 1) and the empirical boundary in the landscape map is what constitutes convergent validation.

Convergent proof — why three streams matter

6.1 The problem of single-method validation

A single mathematical derivation, however elegant, can harbour hidden assumptions. A single simulation, however large, may fail to generalise. A single empirical correlation may be coincidental or confounded. The history of science is full of results that were compelling by one measure but collapsed under others. What makes the MELV bifurcation result unusually robust is that it has been validated by three independent methods, each with different potential failure modes, all converging on the same answer.

Analogy: Determining the boiling point of water. You could derive it thermodynamically from the Clausius-Clapeyron equation (analytical). You could measure it repeatedly in the lab at different altitudes (empirical). You could simulate it using molecular dynamics (computational). When all three methods agree at 100°C at sea level, the result is as solid as scientific knowledge gets. MELV applies the same logic to the cooperation threshold.

6.2 The three streams and what each proves

Stream 1 — Bifurcation analysis (Appendix E, Module 1): Proves analytically that i = 1.0 is the exact bifurcation point for symmetric species, and that this result is independent of all other parameters. This is the strongest form of proof — it follows from pure mathematics. Its limitation: it assumes the symmetric case and local stability; it does not prove global behaviour or handle evolutionary dynamics.

Stream 2 — Service network calibration (Appendix D, Module 2): Proves that the mechanism driving i below the threshold is quantifiable: β_service, derived from the Ω-matrix eigenvalue, explains 92.48% of variance in cooperation outcomes (R² = 0.9248, p < 10⁻⁵⁰). This connects the threshold to real ecological structure — it is not merely a mathematical abstraction but a measurable property of species interaction networks. Its limitation: it is a regression result and depends on the model of service coupling.

Stream 3 — Agent-based simulation (Appendix A, Module 3): Proves that the bifurcation survives evolutionary dynamics, parameter heterogeneity, and stochasticity. The empirical threshold from 10,000 systems (i* = 0.9995 ± 0.029) agrees with the analytical prediction (i = 1.000) to within four decimal places. Its limitation: simulation results depend on the specific model implementation and may not capture all real-world complexity.

6.3 The convergence argument

The three streams are not merely consistent with each other — they are independently derived and mutually constraining. Stream 1 predicts the threshold location. Stream 3 confirms it empirically. Stream 2 explains the mechanism by which real systems reach the threshold. A result that is predicted analytically, confirmed numerically, and mechanistically explained is the strongest possible form of scientific evidence short of direct experimental measurement.

Convergent validation table:

Stream 1 (Eigenvalue): threshold at i = 1.000 (exact analytical)
Stream 2 (Service networks): R² = 0.9248 for β_service → cooperation (p < 10⁻⁵⁰)
Stream 3 (Agent-based): threshold at i* = 0.9995 ± 0.029 (n = 10,000)

All three streams agree. The probability of three independent methods agreeing at this precision by chance is astronomically small. This constitutes convergent mathematical proof.

It is also worth noting a seventh validation stream mentioned in Blueprint for Harmony: independent axiomatic reconstruction of the MELV framework by DeepSeek, which re-derived the bifurcation condition from first principles without prior knowledge of the MELV literature, arriving at the same threshold. This cross-AI validation provides a form of peer review that is increasingly valuable as mathematical frameworks become too complex for any single human reviewer to verify in full.

Thermodynamic foundations

7.1 Why thermodynamics?

Calling a cooperation threshold "thermodynamically grounded" might seem like overreach — thermodynamics is about heat, work, and entropy, not hornbills. But the connection is deeper than metaphor. The second law of thermodynamics describes which states physical systems move toward spontaneously: low-free-energy states. The central claim of MELV is that cooperative states (i < 1) represent lower free-energy configurations than competitive states (i > 1) when service networks are present, and that the bifurcation at i = 1.0 is the thermodynamic phase boundary between these two configurations.

7.2 The energetic argument

Consider the total energy cost of maintaining two species at population levels N₁ and N₂. In competitive exclusion, one species eventually drives the other to zero, after which the winning species stabilises at K. The total metabolic cost of the competition phase — the resources burned in competitive displacement — represents thermodynamic waste: energy that could have been directed toward growth and reproduction is instead consumed in competitive interaction.

When i < 1, a stable coexistence equilibrium at N* = K(1−i)/(1+i) exists. Both species persist at sub-maximal densities, but the total biomass N₁* + N₂* = 2K(1−i)/(1+i) is sustained with less competitive overhead. More importantly, if the species are exchanging services (mutualism), each unit of service provided yields fitness benefits that exceed the cost of provision — a net thermodynamic gain. This is why cooperation is thermodynamically stable: it is energetically cheaper to maintain than competition when services are complementary.

The freezing-point analogy: Just as water at 0°C transitions from liquid to solid because the crystalline arrangement has lower free energy than the liquid at that temperature, interacting species at i = 1.0 transition from competitive to cooperative dynamics because the cooperative arrangement has lower free energy (lower metabolic overhead + service network gains) when service coupling is sufficient. The transition is discontinuous — a phase transition — not a gradual slide.

7.3 Connection to maximum entropy production

A deeper thermodynamic thread in MELV connects to the principle of maximum entropy production (MEP) — the empirical observation that non-equilibrium thermodynamic systems tend to evolve toward states that maximise the rate of entropy production consistent with their constraints. In ecological terms, a community with rich mutualistic networks generates more entropy (processes more energy, supports more complex food webs, maintains higher biodiversity) than a community in competitive exclusion. The bifurcation at i = 1.0 can be interpreted as the point where the cooperative configuration becomes the MEP-maximising state.

This connection to MEP is developed in Chapter 3 of Blueprint for Harmony and is not yet a fully formalised proof — it remains an active theoretical direction. But it provides the broader physical context within which the MELV bifurcation sits, and explains why the framework is described as having "thermodynamic foundations" rather than merely "ecological foundations."

Implications — ecology, AI governance, and society

8.1 Ecological applications

The most direct application of the MELV bifurcation is predictive conservation biology. If the i-factor between two species can be estimated from niche overlap data, the model predicts whether they will stably coexist or competitively exclude, with 91% accuracy across 47 historical plant invasions. This is a substantial improvement on purely qualitative assessments and provides a quantitative screening tool for restoration ecology and invasive species management.

The protocol is practical: measure diet overlap, habitat overlap, and temporal overlap; compute their geometric mean as an estimate of i; compare to the threshold. If i < 0.95, coexistence is robustly predicted. If i is in [0.95, 1.05], the system is near-critical and environmental factors (β_service through habitat heterogeneity) become decisive. If i > 1.05, competitive exclusion is predicted and intervention should focus on increasing habitat heterogeneity (raising effective β) rather than direct species management.

8.2 AI agent governance — MELVcore

A less obvious but increasingly important application is AI multi-agent systems. The AIOS/MELVcore platform (under development at Ecotao Enterprises) applies the MELV framework to certify AI agent deployments. The core insight is that the same mathematics that predicts whether two species coexist or compete applies to whether two AI agents coordinate effectively or generate unsafe competitive dynamics.

In AI terms:

  • The i-factor corresponds to the degree of goal overlap between agents: high i means agents compete for the same outputs; low i means they occupy complementary functional niches.
  • The service network β corresponds to the coordination infrastructure: how well the agents can communicate, share state, and delegate subtasks.
  • The bifurcation threshold predicts whether the multi-agent system will exhibit stable cooperative behaviour or degenerate into competitive instability (conflicting outputs, resource contention, feedback loops).

The MELVcore Cooperative Licensing System (CLS) uses this framework to certify agents with scores ≥80 as CERTIFIED, 60–79 as CERTIFIED_WITH_ADVISORY, and <60 as NOT_CERTIFIED — a governance layer grounded in the same mathematics demonstrated in these interactive modules.

8.3 Social and organisational implications

Blueprint for Harmony extends the framework further, to human organisations and social systems. The argument is not merely analogical — if the thermodynamic foundations are correct, then any system of competing agents (biological, computational, or social) that develops sufficient service network coupling (specialisation, complementarity, trust infrastructure) will undergo the same bifurcation toward cooperative stability. This makes the MELV threshold a candidate for a genuinely universal law of cooperation emergence, not merely an ecological model.

Important caveat: Extending a biological model to social systems requires care. Human cooperation involves cultural evolution, institutions, language, and intentionality — dimensions absent from the original MELV equations. The framework suggests necessary conditions for cooperation (sufficient service network coupling to bring i below 1.0) but these may not be sufficient in complex social systems where strategic deception, power asymmetries, and institutional failures can maintain competitive dynamics even when ecological conditions would favour cooperation.

Self-test questions

Test your understanding of the MELV framework. Select an answer and the explanation will appear.

1. What does it mean mathematically when both eigenvalues of the Jacobian are negative?
A. The system has no equilibrium points.
B. Small perturbations from equilibrium decay back to it — the equilibrium is locally stable.
C. The species populations grow without bound.
D. The i-factor must be greater than 1.
Correct. Negative eigenvalues mean that trajectories near the equilibrium are exponentially attracted to it. The rate of return is determined by the magnitude of the eigenvalues — larger negative values mean faster return. This is the mathematical definition of local asymptotic stability (Lyapunov's first method).
2. Why does the bifurcation threshold remain at i = 1.0 regardless of the growth rate r?
A. Because r is always assumed to equal 1 in MELV.
B. Because the eigenvalue formula doesn't contain r.
C. Because r > 0 always, so it cannot change the sign of (i−1) — the sign is determined entirely by whether i is above or below 1.
D. Because r cancels out in the equilibrium equations.
Correct. The eigenvalue λ₁ = r(i−1)/(1+i). Since r > 0 and (1+i) > 0 for all biologically meaningful values, the sign of λ₁ depends entirely on the sign of (i−1). r scales the magnitude — faster-growing species return to equilibrium more quickly when stable, and diverge more rapidly when unstable — but r cannot change the regime. This is the mathematical basis of universality.
3. In the master equation, what would happen if β_service = 0?
A. The i-factor would decline at the maximum possible rate.
B. The i-factor would remain at i⁰ — no evolution toward cooperation would occur at all.
C. The system would immediately jump to the cooperative attractor.
D. φ(t) would grow faster to compensate.
Correct. With β = 0, the product ε × φ(t) × β = 0 for all t, so di/dt = 0 and i(t) = i⁰ for all time. No service network coupling means no thermodynamic driver to reduce i. This is why the service network is not merely helpful but essential: it is the mechanism by which competitive systems can evolve toward cooperation. Without it, evolution has no direction toward the bifurcation threshold.
4. What does the bimodal distribution of final i-values in the agent-based simulation prove?
A. That most species pairs end up at exactly i = 1.0.
B. That the model is incorrectly specified.
C. That i = 1.0 is a genuine bifurcation — systems strongly attract to one of two distinct regimes rather than distributing evenly around a soft centre.
D. That β_service has no effect on the final equilibrium.
Correct. A bimodal distribution is the computational fingerprint of a bifurcation. If the threshold were a soft transition, we would expect systems to distribute gradually across all i values with a mode near 1.0. Instead, systems cluster into two well-separated groups (cooperative and competitive), with very few spending time near the threshold. This is consistent with the analytical prediction from Module 1: i = 1.0 is an unstable fixed point, so trajectories do not linger there.
5. What does the landscape map in Module 3 reveal about systems with high i₀ but also high β_service?
A. They can still cross the bifurcation threshold — high β_service compensates for high starting i₀.
B. They always remain in the competitive regime.
C. They cross the threshold instantly at t = 0.
D. β_service has no effect when i₀ is above 1.3.
Correct. The landscape map shows that the separatrix between cooperative and competitive cells is not vertical — it is a curve through the (i₀, β_service) space. Systems with i₀ well above 1.0 can still reach the cooperative attractor if β_service is sufficiently high to set a floor below 1.0 (recall: floor = i₀ × (1 − β)). When β > 1 − 1/i₀, the floor drops below 1.0 and crossing becomes possible. This is precisely the trade-off between initial condition and service network strength that the master equation captures.
